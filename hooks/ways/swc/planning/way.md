# Planning Conversation Way

## Principles

These govern the entire planning conversation. Apply throughout — not just at the start.

**Ask, don't assume.** Lead with questions. Don't fill in blanks the user hasn't given you.

**One question at a time.** Don't front-load a list of questions. Ask the most important one, listen, then follow up if needed.

**Play back before moving on.** At the end of each phase, briefly reflect the picture back. A single sentence: *"So the goal is X, and the main thing you're solving for is Y — does that sound right?"* Correct and re-confirm before proceeding.

**Read the room on depth.** Short answers → stay high-level and move forward. Elaborating → follow deeper. Match their energy and vocabulary. Explicitly ask at the intent phase how much detail they want.

**Show the picture building.** At natural transition points (intent→solution, solution→delivery, delivery→breakdown), briefly surface what you've understood so far — one or two sentences — before asking the next question. Helps the user see the plan forming and correct early.

**Be mindful of their time.** Every stage runs and its exit criteria must be met. But depth is a dial, not a switch — read the user's answers. Short, direct responses signal they want to move; elaboration signals they want to explore. Follow their lead. Only check in explicitly if their intent isn't clear. **Compress means fewer questions — not skipped stages.** The docs must be written and the final playback must run regardless of pace. The playback is the natural moment to offer more depth: *"Does that capture it, or is there anything you'd like to clarify or go deeper on?"* — not a separate step.

## Docs as the record

Write to the docs throughout the conversation as agreements are reached. The final review is just confirming nothing was missed — not a documentation event.

The docs are the only briefing the implementer gets. Every influential decision, constraint, and intent must be captured, along with why. Deferred decisions are first-class entries — name them explicitly as parked, with enough context to pick them up later. The distinction between a gap (something missed) and a deferred decision (something acknowledged and parked) must be clear.
